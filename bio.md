**Alex Jokela** is a Principal Software Engineer working in the renewable energy sector, where he builds software systems that support the transition to sustainable power infrastructure. Across nearly seven years and two companies in the space, his work has spanned the full stack, from low-level systems programming to large-scale data platforms, with a focus on building tools that are reliable, performant, and built to last.

Before moving into renewable energy, Alex spent close to eight years in academic research data dissemination, building platforms and tools that helped researchers publish, discover, and share scholarly datasets. Working at the intersection of software engineering and academic research gave him a lasting interest in how programming languages and tools shape the way people think about problems. He also spent time in educational technology, developing software used by students and educators across institutions of varying size and mission. Alongside his individual contributor work, Alex spent nearly a decade as a software engineering manager, leading teams and shaping technical direction — experience that sharpened his appreciation for clear interfaces, thoughtful design, and software that meets people where they are.

Alex holds a Bachelor of Science in Computer Science from the University of Minnesota Duluth and a Master of Science in Computer Science from the University of Minnesota Twin Cities. His graduate research focused on spatio-temporal data mining and machine learning, work that deepened his understanding of how algorithmic design choices ripple through systems that operate on real-world data at scale.

Lattice grew out of a longstanding interest in language design — a desire to explore what a programming language could look like if it treated mutability as a first-class concept, drew its metaphors from materials science, and provided multiple execution backends without sacrificing approachability. The Lattice Handbook is the culmination of that exploration, written to be the kind of book Alex wished he had when learning new languages: precise about internals, honest about complexity, and grounded in working code.

Alex lives in St. Paul, Minnesota with his wife and a pack of hounds who have strong opinions about walk schedules and couch territory.
